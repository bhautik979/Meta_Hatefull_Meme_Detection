ABSTRACT
The proliferation of harmful information, such as hate speech and online harassment, has increased in recent 
years due to social media ’ s explosive expansion. Using the Facebook Hate Meme Dataset (FBHM), we create a 
reliable model in this work for identifying multimodal hate speech on online platforms. To effectively address 
class imbalance and improve classification accuracy, our hybrid model combines ResNet for image processing 
with RoBERTa for text analysis, leveraging Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) and Incre -
mental Principal Component Analysis (PCA) combined with adversarial machine learning techniques. The 
combination of Incremental PCA ’ s dimensionality reduction and SMOTE ’ s synthetic sample creation produces a 
potent combination that enhances the training dataset and maximizes feature representation, resulting in 
improved online content moderation techniques. We achieved an accuracy of 81.80 %, and a Macro-F1 score of 
81.53 % on the FBHM dataset which represents an 18 % improvement in accuracy over the base model. These 
results provide significant novel insights into this important field of study by demonstrating the potential of 
adversarial approaches in creating reliable models for automated hate speech identification that can help create a 
safer online environment and can significantly reduce the emotional burden on human content moderators by 
handling the contents quickly and accurately. This study highlights the mutually beneficial effect of combining 
SMOTE and incremental PCA, demonstrating how they improve the model ’ s ability to correct class imbalance 
and boost performance. The source code and dataset are publicly available on GitHub to facilitate reproducibility 
and further research. Link to the code and dataset below:
https://github.com/ludivintchokote/HatePostDetection
1. Introduction
The rise of social media platforms has led to an increase in the 
propagation of harmful content, including hate speech and online 
harassment. Effectively identifying this type of content has become an 
important area of research, especially regarding multimodal data that 
encompasses both text and images. Online hate ( Waqas, Salminen, Jung, 
Almerekhi & Jansen BJ, 2019 ) encompasses a variety of prejudiced 
expressions aimed at individuals or groups based on characteristics such 
as race, religion, gender, and sexual orientation. This type of content can 
manifest in derogatory comments, threats, and dehumanizing language, 
often leading to serious emotional and psychological consequences for 
victims. The impact of online hate ( Barth, Wagner, Raab & Wieg ¨artner, 
2023 ) is exacerbated by its diverse manifestations across various media 
formats, which complicates efforts to address it comprehensively. As the 
digital environment continues to evolve, understanding the dynamics 
and consequences of online hate is essential for fostering a safer online 
community for all users. However, a significant challenge in developing 
effective hate speech detection systems lies in addressing inherent biases 
within the datasets used for training.
The Facebook Hateful Memes dataset ( Kiela, Mohan & Firooz, 2020 ) 
is a pioneering resource developed by Facebook AI to aid researchers in 
the detection of multimodal hate speech, which combines both text and 
images. The dataset suffers from class imbalance, thus presenting a 
shared challenge in machine learning, where examples are not evenly 
distributed among various classes. Imbalanced data presents significant 
challenges for machine learning algorithms, often resulting in biased 
models that underperform on the minority class. Numerous strategies 
( Surani, Domaratzki & Saman, 2024 ) have been suggested to tackle class 
imbalance, yet a considerable research gap remains in effectively

Overall, since communication on social media primarily occurs 
through images and text, the innovative techniques developed for 
detecting online hate content remain vulnerable to multimodal adver -
sarial attacks. These attacks aim to disrupt the classification abilities of 
detection systems, leading to the misclassification of hate content. 
Achieving high accuracy in predictions is important for effective hate 
content detection, as both false positives and false negatives can have 
serious repercussions. Frequent false positives may provoke backlash 
from users who believe their posts are being unjustly suppressed and 
censored, while false negatives can allow hate content to spread un -
checked. The primary goal of this research is to develop a robust 
multimodal model for detecting hate online content, specifically utiliz -
ing the combination of Incremental PCA, SMOTE, and adversarial 
training on the Facebook Hate Meme Dataset to address challenges 
related to class imbalance and adversarial robustness. This combination 
enhances data representation, balances class distributions, and improves 
robustness against adversarial attacks.

deeper into previous works related to multimodal deep learning 
frameworks, adversarial machine learning techniques, dimensionality 
reduction, and class imbalance handling. Section 3 describes the pro -
posed approach, and Section 4 presents the results from the experiments 
conducted and compares them with previous works. Finally, Section 5

2. State of the art
2.1. Multimodal deep learning frameworks
Chhabra et al. ( Chhabra & Vishwakarma, 2023 ) introduced a novel 
architecture that employs multi-scale visual kernels combined with 
knowledge distillation techniques for detecting hateful memes. This 
framework extracts relevant features from visual content while ensuring 
the model learns effectively from visual and textual data sources. Their 
results demonstrate improved performance in identifying memetic hate 
speech compared to conventional methods.
A notable study by Singh et al. ( Singh, Vajrobol & Aggarwal, 2023 ) 
investigates the effectiveness of using XLM-Roberta-base for detecting 
hate speech in multimodal data, particularly in text-embedded images. 
The research utilized a dataset comprising over 4700 images related to 
the Russia-Ukraine conflict and found that XLM-Roberta-base out -
performed other classifiers, achieving an F1 score of 84.62 for identi -
fying hate speech targets. This study emphasizes the importance of 
integrating advanced deep-learning models to improve detection accu -
racy across different content types.
Today, memes represent the most prevalent forms of multimodal 
communication on social media. Although often created with ironic 
intent, they can also convey hateful messages directed at various in -
dividuals or groups. Recent research by Hee et al. ( Hee, Lee & Chong, 
2022 ) suggests that the most effective multimodal approach combines 
textual and visual features utilizing transformer-based architectures, 
such as ViLBERT and Visual-BERT. These methods have been bench -
marked against other unimodal and multimodal strategies, establishing 
them as a baseline for this challenge and underscoring the necessity for 
multimodal analysis in detecting hate content. Despite achieving 
approximately 75 % accuracy, these models still lag behind human 
performance, highlighting the need to develop new machine-learning 
models.
2.2. Adversarial machine learning techniques
Several innovative defense strategies have been proposed in the 
literature to protect hate speech detection models from adversarial at -
tacks. Most of these strategies utilize adversarial training to manage 
perturbations effectively. Tran et al., ( Tran et al., 2020 ) implemented 
adversarial training to defend against Fast Gradient Method (FGM) at -
tacks by augmenting FGM with a customizable and detailed noise 
magnitude, adding noise (perturbation) to misleading samples. The hate 
speech detection model they developed demonstrates a superior un -
derstanding of hate speech datasets, operates 4 – 5 times faster than the 
conventional BERT model, consumes less than one-third of the memory, 
and achieves enhanced classification performance.
Wang et al., ( Wang, Wang, Ke & Wang, 2021 ) introduce a defensive 
tool called TextFirewall designed for sentiment analysis algorithms. This 
tool leverages the disparity between the predictions made by sentiment 
analysis models and the calculated impact value of words, which reflects 
their positive or negative influence on sentiment polarity. TextFirewall 
successfully detects adversarial texts produced by three leading attack 
methods and surpasses earlier defense strategies. Specifically, it ach -
ieves an average accuracy of 90.7 % on the IMDB dataset and 96.9 % on 
the Yelp dataset when countering these attacks. Notably, TextFirewall 
can function as a third-party tool without requiring modifications to the 
target model and is effective against various types of adversarial texts.
Aggarwal et al., ( Aggarwal et al., 2023 ) conducted a case study to 
examine the weaknesses of current hateful meme detection systems in 
the face of external adversarial attacks. They identified nine distinct 
types of attacks to assess how susceptible these detection systems are to 
human-induced adversarial inputs, they discovered that all models 
tested exhibited significant vulnerabilities, with some experiencing up 
to a 10 % decrease in macro-F1 performance under certain conditions. 
To resolve these vulnerabilities, they proposed two countermeasures: 
one based on contrastive learning and the other on adversarial training. 
For the countermeasure involving adversarial training, the model was 
not only trained on existing data points but also on adversarial examples 
created by altering one modality of the input data while keeping another 
constant. Specifically, for image data, they focused on image-region 
features instead of modifying individual pixels.
2.3. Dimensionality reduction and class imbalance handling
Basgall et al., ( Basgall, Hasperue, Naiouf & Fernandez, 2018 ) intro -
duced a fully scalable preprocessing technique (SMOTE-BD) designed to 
solve imbalanced classification in Big Data. They used the SMOTE al -
gorithm, to create new synthetic samples based on the proximity of 
minority class instances. SMOTE-BD is designed to operate indepen -
dently of the number of partitions or processes, thereby enhancing its 
efficiency.
Mohammed et al., ( Mohammed, Hassan & Kadir, 2020 ) used a novel 
dataset called ZADA, which includes medical information from over 
7000 patients in Zakho, Iraq, to build a system to assess and detect pa -
tients with disproportionate diabetes. They diagnosed diabetes using six 
machine learning techniques and they corrected class imbalance by 
using a classification analysis that included the SMOTE resampling 
approach with three normalization strategies. Their study showed that 
the performance of the classification models based on minority class 
distribution was much enhanced by both the resampling and normal -
izing techniques.
Mulla et al., ( Mulla, Demir & Hassan, 2021 ) performed a method that 
combined SMOTE oversampling with PCA to solve imbalance problems 
in high dimensional data. They used Logistic Regression, K-Nearest 
Neighbor, and Decision Tree methods on two separate datasets to 
evaluate their method efficiency and to determine their classifiers ’ 
performance. They used WEKA with the algorithms above to analyze 
raw datasets and converted datasets by PCA, SMOTE, and SMOTE + PCA. 
The results they obtained showed that almost all classification algo -
rithms improved their classification accuracy using PCA, SMOTE, and 
SMOTE + PCA methods, with the SMOTE method giving the most effi -
cient result for data rebalancing and K-Nearest Neighbor having the

To mitigate potential biases present in the FBHM dataset, we 
implemented the following strategies in conjunction with incremental 
PCA and SMOTE: 
• Data augmentation: While SMOTE helps address the class imbalance 
thereby reducing bias towards the majority class, the use of pre- 
trained models (ResNet50 and RoBERTa) can be considered a form 
of transfer learning. These pre-trained models have learned features 
from features from larger datasets, which is beneficial for smaller 
and potentially biased datasets.
• Class weighting: To explicitly reduce bias, we calculated the class 
weights based on the inverse class frequencies in the training data 
and applied them to the loss function. By doing so, we address bias by 
penalizing misclassification more heavily on minority classes
3.2. Model architecture
Classification of data with class imbalance problems as well as 
building robust models against adversarial attacks is a great center of 
interest for researchers in different scientific domains. This study pro -
poses a new method based on combining adversarial learning with 
SMOTE (for rebalancing) and PCA (for dimensionality reduction) to 
enhance the robustness and improve the accuracy of our classification 
model.
Multimodal Neural Network . We proposed an ensemble model that 
combines pre-trained transformer model (RoBERTa) for text processing 
and ResNet-50 for image processing. Fig. 1 shows an overview of the 
ensemble model used.
The pretrained models are combined using a simple feedforward 
neural network. The aim is to enable the model learn features from both 
forms of data and fuse them into a unified representation as shown in 
Fig. 1 . Studies performed by Alec et al., ( Alec et al., 2021 ) revealed deep 
CNN as a state-of-the-art method in image classification tasks. We 
choose ResNet50 because it uses pretrained deep CNN to extract visual 
features. We used google vision API to extract the text from the memes, 
the extracted text is then passed to RoBERTa to learn the textual fea -
tures. RoBERTa ’ s main innovation is that it uses a more robust and 
efficient pretraining approach, which allows it to obtain better results on 
most NLP tasks. We concatenated the combined features from both 
modalities and passed it through a fully connected layer to produce class 
scores.
3.3. Training procedure
As shown in Fig. 2 , the method starts with using the original dataset, 
and then we started by checking if it is imbalanced (i.e. if the number of 
labels in the hateful class is not equals to the number of labels in the non- 
hateful class for the training set). If the training set is imbalanced, then 
we balanced it by applying incremental PCA (to first reduce the 
dimensionality of the data)and SMOTE method. Else, we proceeded to 
creating adversarial dataloaders to be able to add adversarial examples 
in the training set to be used during the training process. For the 
adversarial examples used during training, we mainly focused on the 
model-independent attacks which can be introduced by humans with 
little technical expertise. Some examples of these attacks are developed 
later in another section of this research. A random number between 1 
and 10 was generated for each batch of data, if the number generated

For scenarios where we cannot predetermine the internal behavior of

• Blur: Applying a blur effect to an image reduces its clarity and

• Color Jitter: Modifying the color balance or intensity of an image can

• Stripes: adding stripes or patterns to an image can disrupt the

• Random Noise: Adding random noise to an input image can disrupt 
clarity and mislead the model.
• Shuffle pixels: Randomly rearranging pixels in an image can signif -
icantly alter its appearance and challenge the model to correctly 
classify it.
4. Implementation and results
In this section, we described the environment in which the study was 
carried out and we presented the results obtained for the classification of 
memes into hate and non-hateful content.
4.1. Dataset
We used the Facebook Hate Meme (FBHM) dataset ( Price, 2020 ) 
provided by Facebook AI. The dataset contains 10,000 memes collected 
from Getty images and has been annotated in a semi-artificially manner 
by benign confounders. The dataset is made up of five classes of memes 
which are multimodal hate, unimodal hate, benign image, benign text, 
and random not-hateful examples. We labeled the first four classes of 
memes as hateful and the rest as non-hateful. The dataset was split into 
85 % training, 5 % development, and 10 % test sets.
The content types of the dataset include racist, and sexist memes and 
also contents that incites violence or targets protected categories like 
religion, gender, and sexual orientation. Table 2 describes the dataset 
size and label distribution
4.2. Experimental setup
Image Branch . We resized and normalized the input image before 
passing it to our pretrained ResNet50 model. The output of the last 
convolutional layer of the ResNet50 model was extracted as the image 
feature map with a tensor shape of (16, 2048).
Text Branch . We extracted the text from the meme, and divided it 
into subwords through a process called tokenization. We accomplished 
it using the WordPiece algorithm, which divides words into sub-words 
based on their frequency and patterns of co-occurrence. We used a 
learned embedding matrix to subsequently transform each token into a 
numerical representation known as embedding. These embeddings 
encapsulate the semantic meaning of each token. We passed the text 
tensor of shape (16, 768) through the RoBERTa encoder to enable the 
model to capture the contextual relationships between the tokens.
Classifier and Training . To obtain the multimodal comprehensive 
features, we concatenated the image and text tensors along the feature 
dimension to create a new tensor of shape (16, 2 × 768). We passed the 
concatenated tensor through two fully connected layers with ReLU 
activation functions, followed by a final fully connected layer with no

5. Conclusions and future works
In this paper, we highlighted the need to build robust systems to be 
able to effectively detect hate content online as it has become common 
to spread hate through images and text on social media and masking the 
posts to evade online detectors. We tackle this challenge by addressing 
both data imbalances and model vulnerabilities. We uniquely combine 
SMOTE, to mitigate class imbalance, with incremental PCA, which offers 
memory-efficient dimensionality reduction which is critical for handling 
large, multimodal datasets like the Facebook Hateful Memes (FBHM) 
dataset. This combination enabled our multimodal model to achieve an 
accuracy of 81.80 % on the validation set, thereby outperforming 
existing benchmarks. Beyond accuracy, we acknowledge the ethical 
imperative of building models that are resilient to adversarial manipu -
lation. Recognizing that even non-technical attacks can significantly 
affect the models ’ performance, this work provides a practical approach 
to fortifying hate post detection in real-world scenarios. We stored the 
model ’ s prediction on the test set in a JSON file for human-level judg -
ment and we discussed some predictions it made.
We noticed that the adoption of incremental PCA proved to be 
particularly beneficial, offering the scalability and adaptability essential 
for modern machine learning workflows dealing with ever-growing data 
streams. To enhance model training by ensuring that both images and 
text features are appropriately scaled and reduced before further pro -
cessing, we used incremental PCA as a crucial preprocessing step to 
enable efficient handling of multimodal data while addressing class
